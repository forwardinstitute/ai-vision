This is a reset moment.

The default organisational response to AI is to use it to do what we already do, faster. That's a mistake.

The Times · 12 April 2026 Britain lags global leaders in making the most of AI innovation "The companies getting the biggest returns from AI globally aren't just doing more of the same — they are reinventing their businesses." Leigh Bates, Global Risk AI Leader, PwC UK. British businesses derive only 27% of revenue from products that didn't exist three years ago, vs 43% for global leaders.

AI is rooted in the same systems we're trying to fix. If we're not careful, it just accelerates them. Efficiency is seductive. It feels like progress, but it is often just speeding up the same outcomes. And in doing so, it strengthens the gravitational pull of the average, making it even harder for leaders to break patterns, processes and behaviours.

Last year, we moved from Salesforce to Notion. Not to do things the same way, but to do things differently. It was adopted. It was a lesson: when the tool fits the culture, people move. AI is the same principle at a much larger scale. It asks us to reconsider what our systems are actually for.

For Forward Institute, that question is specific: how do we take ten years of experience, unprecedented access to senior leaders, and a belief system that already sets us apart, and make all of it dramatically more powerful?

Our core argument is that leadership is authorship. The creative and courageous use of power to shape a better future. We ask leaders to stop being spectators and start being authors. AI is the most powerful tool for authorship that has ever existed. If we don't use it to put that argument into leaders' hands, not just their ears, we are guilty of exactly the managerial logic we're telling them to reject.

That's why this matters for us specifically. Not because AI is new, or because everyone else is doing it. But because this technology makes our founding belief actionable at a scale we've never had.

The majority of people find it almost impossible to imagine an incrementally better future.

We can imagine robots blasting buildings to smithereens. We cannot imagine healthcare where cancer screenings take thirty seconds, or tax returns take twenty-five. Our tools will be in the hands of some of the most senior people in the UK. Their job is to create this new world. Our job is to move them towards the possible.

That, in the end, is what we ask of the leaders we work with. To go first. To move before the path is fully clear. It would be strange to ask anything less of ourselves.

We have already proven this works.

Lumen exists. It sits within Compass, our leadership platform. Together, they could change how we work, how we sell, and how leadership teams experience us.

Lumen collects reflections throughout a day of workshops or a programme of events, synthesises them, and helps leaders rearrange and clarify their thinking. It did three things we expected.

1
It helped individual leaders process the sessions and stimulus in the room.
2
It helped organisations understand the sentiment in the room more clearly.
3
It created a new kind of insight. We can now see sentiment, frustrations, and patterns that conventional delivery could never surface.

But there were impacts we were not anticipating in the same way.

4
AI is enabling us to do new things. Lumen would previously have taken upwards of £90k to build. We are not replacing capability, but adding it.
5
Internal leadership saw the power of what we could do. Belief shifted.
6
It indicated a change in our commercial model. Offering something unique has helped secure work with some of the biggest organisations in the country.

What would we do if we really meant it?

If everything goes right.

What becomes true.

01

AI products become a distinct strand of commercial income.

New revenue. New reach. New relationships with organisations that may never attend a programme but will use our tools.

02

Every leader gets something different.

Not every leader needs the same thing. Some need support, some need provocation, some need to be unsettled. Our tools learn preferences and adjust their posture: more challenge, more stretch, more pressure where it is needed. The tool extends what our facilitators can do, capturing and synthesising thoughts even from those less inclined to speak openly in traditional forums.

03

What we learn in one place changes how we show up in another.

What we learn on one side of the organisation powers the other. Insight from programmes informs commercial conversations. Sentiment from leadership tools sharpens our advisory work. Nothing resets. Everything accumulates.

04

We start to see what nobody else can see.

We own the interface where leadership thinking happens. That gives us something nobody else has: a live, continuous view of how senior leaders across sectors are making sense of the world, where they get stuck, and when they act. Not a survey. Not a report commissioned after the fact. A real-time picture of how leadership is actually changing, drawn from thousands of honest reflections. That view becomes our authority.

05

We know what we think, and we can prove it.

Our intellectual property becomes structured, versioned, and anchored. Fixed points of view that evolve through deliberate iteration, forming the underpinning of everything we build, say, and sell.

None of this works without trust.

People will only use our products if we remain credible.

Integrity is a USP in so much of what we do. Leaders share their thinking with us because they trust us. That trust has been built over ten years, in rooms, through relationships, face to face.

Now the opportunity to do more is easy. AI makes it simple to collect, aggregate, and analyse at scale. That is exactly why this moment is dangerous. Our values and our credibility hinge on how we make choices around data usage. What we collect, how we store it, who sees it, and what we do with it.

Every decision we make about data must be a decision about trust. If we get this right, it becomes our strongest differentiator. If we get it wrong, nothing else in this document matters.

The path from here.

Year One / Leverage

Move fast. We already have what most organisations spend years building.

We have the access, the network, the trust, and a platform that already works. We build the infrastructure by building the products.

Compass evolves from a session-by-session tool into something a leader can return to. Something that adapts to them, understands their context, and supports them through change.

Data architecture and version-controlled IP are built into everything from day one. Cross-programme insight starts surfacing patterns immediately.

We publish our first leadership sentiment analysis. The commercial model begins to shift: organisations start buying our tools and our perspective, not just our programmes.

Beyond Year One / Position

Become the organisation that understands how leadership is actually changing.

I'm being honest here: a detailed three-year roadmap would be fiction. The tools I'm building with now didn't exist in our vocabulary twelve months ago. Planning in detail for thirty-six months is counterintuitive when the landscape shifts every quarter. What I can describe is the position we're building towards, and commit to navigating there in the open.

The signal compounds. Forward Institute holds a real-time view of leadership thinking across sectors, drawn from thousands of interactions with senior leaders. We generate insight that shapes how organisations and leaders evolve. Digital products are a material part of revenue. Our published perspective is referenced, cited, sought out. Our access, trust, and technology create a position that no other organisation can replicate.

How we will know this is working.

Organisations pay for our tools without attending a programme.

We publish a leadership sentiment view that people actively seek out.

Our tools are used by leaders outside of facilitated environments.

Insight from one organisation clearly shapes work in another.

Our perspective is referenced, not just heard.

The risk.

That we treat AI the way most organisations will. That we hand people struts and pillars to maintain the tunnels that already exist, when we should be giving them pickaxes to dig new ones. The tools matter, but they are not the prize. The prize is the reinvention of how we work and the work we can do.

If we stop at building useful things and never become something fundamentally different, we will fail to do what we're pushing our leaders to. To courageously and creatively lean into the biggest problems of our time.

What this requires. Honestly.

I am not a traditional leader. I don't have a conventional background. But I have conviction, and I believe in what we're doing.

My identity needs to shift. I have operated as a fixer. To lead this, I need to become a driver. I can do this. The tools we produce are our chance to get our thinking into the hands of every leader we work with. That only works if the thinking is consistent.

I need space and time to make mess before I tidy it up. Just as rehearsals are safe spaces to try things out before the audience come in, I think what the last 18 months has shown is that in the exploration, the path ahead can emerge.

I'm not coming with a budget request at this stage. I'm coming with a direction. If the direction is right, the investment conversation follows.
